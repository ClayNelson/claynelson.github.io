Where the surplus lands

A working model for how economic value migrates across the software supply chain as inference commoditizes. June 2026.

The instinct, when you watch inference cost fall, is to model a cost curve: software gets cheaper to build, margins compress, everyone benefits a little. That is the boring version, and it is wrong in the way that matters. Falling inference cost is not value destruction. It is value relocation. Every dollar that leaves the metered token reappears somewhere else in the chain. The only interesting question — the one that prices stocks — is where.

This is the commoditization-of-complements move. When one layer commoditizes, margin migrates to the adjacent layer that is not yet commoditized and that the now-cheap layer makes more valuable. So the variables below are not independent cost inputs. They are competitors for the same migrating surplus. That competition is the spine of the model.

The chain, as five layers competing for one pool

Restate the usual list — cost of inference, model size, edge compute, harness maturity — not as costs but as the things that move surplus:

Edge silicon. When a good-enough model runs on an amortized workstation, inference converts from opex (metered, per-token, captured by whoever sells tokens) to capex (fixed, depreciating, captured once by the silicon). This is the most under-priced shift, because it requires believing the small-model threshold gets crossed for real development work.

Metered inference & the frontier model. The middle. This is the only layer that cannot win: falling cost is pure margin erosion for whoever sells the token, because the token is the product. Via negativa — its value lives in what it cannot defend once the thing it sells deflates.

Harness & orchestration. What converts unreliable per-step inference into reliable multi-step work. Errors don’t cancel; they stack. 95% per-step reliability over 50 steps is 7.7% success. The harness exists to fight that compounding, and its maturity is measured in how many autonomous steps it can chain before a human must intervene.

Verification & compliance evidence. The deflation-immune pool. Compliance value does not fall when tokens get cheap. In a regulated, safety-critical context the evidence that the chain succeeded is itself the product — and whoever holds the audit-grade record captures margin immune to inference commoditization.

The barbell

Take the small-model thesis and the harness-as-bottleneck seriously and the model predicts a barbell. Surplus migrates down to commoditized edge silicon — captured once, as capex — and up to the verification, trust, and orchestration layer — captured continuously, immune to token deflation. It gets squeezed out of the middle: metered frontier inference sold by the token for general development work.

The middle is where consensus currently prices the most value. That gap — between where consensus puts the value and where the structure of the chain says it lands — is the trade.

realized_work = harness_maturity × (0.4 + 0.6 × trust_accrual)

One relationship in the model deserves singling out, because it is the most testable. A brilliant harness with no trust behind it does not convert: the term above means realized autonomous work is gated by trust, which compounds slowly and collapses fast. Crank harness maturity alone and watch how little moves until trust rises with it. Trust is the rate limiter on the largest surplus pool in the chain.

Announced vs. demonstrated. This is a Stage 1 abstraction, and it earns the same skepticism I’d apply to anyone else’s claim. The shape of the relationships reflects a deliberate thesis; the specific coefficients are placeholders chosen to make the dynamics legible, not estimated from data. And the model treats total chain surplus as fixed — it asks only how the pool is divided, not how it grows as cheaper inference expands the total amount of software built. That Jevons expansion is real and belongs in Stage 2, where the coefficients get real ranges and the layers can be tested against actual names.

Why this is the leverage question

If the audit-evidence layer is genuinely the most defensible margin in the chain — the one pool that does not erode when tokens go to zero — then the strategic contest is over who holds that record. The thesis underneath the whole model is that the commit chain could become the system of record for software development in an agentic world, rather than remaining downstream of the tools that generate the work. Whoever holds the evidence holds the deflation-immune tip of the barbell.
