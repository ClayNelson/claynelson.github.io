Frameworks

The intellectual apparatus underneath the writing — the concepts that travel between essays, keynotes, and customer conversations.

The Trust Boundary

The line between systems your organization can attest to and systems it cannot. You cannot sign what you did not observe. The architectural test for any AI strategy in a regulated context.

Three-Ring Governance

Mission, protocol, emergence. The protocol ring — gates, traces, capability thresholds — is where most multi-agent systems fail, because emergence is asked to fill what protocol should hold.

The Capability Threshold

Capability threshold determines autonomy threshold. A principle that generalizes from AI governance to family operating systems to development workflows.

Intellectual throughline

The essays above, the Dochkina findings, Haklo’s meeting architecture, the automotive trust-boundary argument, and enterprise multi-team coordination are expressions of one question: in a system that includes both humans and autonomous agents, what determines which decisions require a gate?

The three-ring model is my current answer. Ring 1 holds mission and values. Ring 2 holds protocol: approval gates, handoff mechanisms, capability thresholds, reasoning traces that let downstream agents understand why a decision was made. Ring 3 is where agents work autonomously within those constraints.

The failure mode in almost every multi-agent system I’ve studied is that Ring 2 work gets pushed into Ring 3: emergence tries to fill what protocol should hold. This produces systems that are locally coherent and globally incoherent. The fix isn’t more constraints. It’s better Ring 2 design.

The capability threshold is the autonomy threshold. That principle applies whether you’re designing an AI governance framework, a family operating system, a development workflow that coordinates three AI agents and a human founder, or a compliance control plane for safety-critical automotive software.
