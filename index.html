Current research threads

AI governance and the coordination gate problem

Where do oversight mechanisms belong in a multi-agent system? Most AI safety discussion focuses on capability constraints. I'm more interested in protocol design: how you structure the layer that coordinates agents without turning the human into the bottleneck. I've been developing a three-ring constitutional framework and testing it against empirical evidence from the Dochkina self-organizing agents paper (arXiv:2603.28990, March 2026), which found that self-organizing LLM agents outperform designed hierarchical structures — but only when the constitutional layer is intact.

Governing intelligence across multi-agent development workflows

When you work across Claude, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, and GitHub simultaneously, you experience a structural problem: reasoning degrades at each boundary crossing. The intent that generates a decision lives in conversation. By the time it reaches code, only acceptance criteria survive. I'm sketching an architecture — a protocol layer I'm calling governing intelligence — that preserves reasoning provenance across agent handoffs. The diagrams below are the working model.

The core claim: capability threshold determines autonomy threshold. Ring 2, the protocol layer, is where that threshold gets encoded. In most systems right now, Ring 2 is absent. The human fills the gap. That is both the problem and the opportunity.

Family as the original coordination design problem

I'm building Haklo, a weekly meeting ritual app for families. The design challenge is structurally identical to the enterprise coordination problem: how do you give agents enough autonomy to operate effectively while maintaining coherent intent across time? Haklo's memoir flywheel — decision ancestry chains, values-aware coaching, narrative timeline — is the Ring 2 problem applied at household scale.

The irony I keep returning to: I'm building a system to help families hold the thread of their own story together, while the development workflow for that very system loses the thread at every agent boundary.

The economics of human-in-the-loop AI

LLM token costs grow quadratically with conversation length — every call re-sends the entire history, so input spend scales as N(N+1)/2 across turns. A single well-placed human question that redirects an agent at turn 8 doesn't just save one call; it collapses the entire compound cost of the wrong path. This is the compute-economics argument for Ring 2: good protocol design moves the human upstream, where their intervention is cheapest and the leverage is highest.

Intellectual throughline

The Kirk Problem essay, the Dochkina findings, Haklo's meeting architecture, and enterprise multi-team coordination are four expressions of one question: in a system that includes both humans and autonomous agents, what determines which decisions require a gate?

The three-ring model is my current answer. Ring 1 holds mission and values. Ring 2 holds protocol: approval gates, handoff mechanisms, capability thresholds, reasoning traces that let downstream agents understand why a decision was made. Ring 3 is where agents work autonomously within those constraints.

The failure mode in almost every multi-agent system I've studied is that Ring 2 work gets pushed into Ring 3: emergence tries to fill what protocol should hold. This produces systems that are locally coherent and globally incoherent. The fix isn't more constraints. It's better Ring 2 design.

The capability threshold is the autonomy threshold. That principle applies whether you're designing an AI governance framework, a family operating system, or a development workflow that coordinates three AI agents and a human founder.
