Five forces, from five vantages

Porter is always read from one player’s seat. The same market draws a different threat shape depending on who is sitting in it. June 2026.

A five-forces diagram is not a property of an industry. It is a property of a position in an industry — the view looking outward from one company. GitHub’s forces look nothing like a frontier lab’s. So this model lets you swap the focal player and watch the pentagon redraw, and it wires each force to the same six levers from the surplus model, because the forces are the mechanism by which migrating value actually gets captured or competed away.

Each force pushes its vertex outward as it strengthens. A tight pentagon near the center is a defensible seat; a large one is a squeeze. The point of the swap is the asymmetry: a lever that relieves one player’s supplier pressure raises another’s buyer power. There is no good or bad regime in the abstract — only good or bad relative to where you stand.

What the asymmetry shows

GitHub draws the tightest pentagon, and the reason is structural rather than flattering. Verification weight suppresses three forces at once — buyer power, new entrants, and substitutes all pull inward — because the compliance moat is a single asset defending three sides. Drop the verification lever and all three vertices push out together. That simultaneous movement is the deflation-immune-tip thesis rendered as geometry: one moat, three forces.

Cursor’s pentagon stays stubbornly large on two vertices, honestly so. Supplier power doesn’t collapse even with its own model, because it still routes hard reasoning to frontier models it doesn’t own. And its new-entrant and substitute vertices stay out because it has little verification moat to suppress them. Its real defense is agility — but the model deliberately can’t draw agility as a moat, because speed is a flow, not a stock.

Porter measures structure, not momentum. The framework systematically under-credits agility and over-credits incumbency — so a fast-moving challenger reads weaker than its trajectory suggests, precisely because structural position is what Porter sees and speed is what it can’t. The force intensities here, and the driver weights shown in the tooltips, are theses about how the levers translate into competitive pressure, set by hand and contestable. Not investment advice.
