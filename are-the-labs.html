Are the labs capturing the value?

The frontier model companies look like they own developer tooling right now. Whether that capture is durable is a different question. June 2026.

Right now the frontier model companies look like they are capturing nearly all the value in developer tooling. The valuations are real, the attention is real, and a strategy that pretends otherwise isn’t worth reading. The question worth asking is not who is winning today. It is whether today’s capture is the durable kind — or the kind that competes away.

That distinction is the whole argument. It is easy to confuse two very different things when you see a layer of the stack commanding enormous valuations. Either the market believes that layer has a durable moat — value it can defend — or the market is pricing growth into a layer that is actually deflating, value that will compete away as the layer commoditizes. These look identical on a chart of revenue. They are opposite as positions.

What the frontier layer looks like, economically

Three signals tell you which kind of value capture you are looking at, and for the frontier models all three point the same way. The models substitute for one another more easily every quarter. The price per unit of intelligence — per token — is collapsing. And the leading position changes hands every few months. Those are the signatures of a commoditizing layer, not a defended one. A business can post extraordinary revenue while its core product deflates and its lead stays contestable; that is a fundamentally weaker position than the same revenue sitting behind a moat, even though the income statements may look similar.

None of that means the labs are in trouble. It means the value they capture today is the kind that has to be re-won continuously, against well-funded rivals, while the price of the thing they sell falls. Extraordinary, and fragile at the same time.

The pattern, from earlier platform shifts

The layer that captures value early in a platform shift is rarely the layer that holds it. In the personal-computer era the enormous early value sat in the hardware and the operating system; durable value migrated up to the applications and the distribution channels. In the cloud era, raw compute and storage became commodities sold by the unit, metered and price-competed, while the defensible margin moved up to managed services, the developer platform, and the workflow that locked customers in.

The pattern is consistent enough to state as a principle: in a platform shift, raw capability commoditizes, and durable value migrates to whoever controls the workflow, the integration, and the trust layer. The frontier model is today’s raw capability — astonishing, expensive to produce, and on the same path that compute and operating systems already walked. The argument is not that the labs are doomed. It is that the value is moving, and an executive should build around where it is going, not where it sits this quarter.

If you believe value migrates up to workflow and trust, the strategic question for a buyer of development AI is not “which model do I standardize on.” That optimizes the deflating layer. It is “where do I want to own the workflow, the integration, and the record of what my agents actually did.”

What it means if you are buying development AI

The instinct of most executives is to treat the model as the strategic relationship: pick the best one, negotiate the token price, lock it in. The economics say that is anchoring your strategy to the layer with the least durable value — the one getting cheaper and more substitutable every month. The sharper posture is to separate what is deflating from what is accruing, and to structure supplier relationships accordingly.

Keep the deflating layers loosely coupled. For raw inference — the model itself — your leverage as a buyer is highest and your switching costs should be lowest. Architect so you can swap model vendors without re-platforming, negotiate short, and treat the unit price as a number that will keep falling without your help. A vendor asking you to commit for years to a specific model is asking you to bet against deflation in the one place deflation is most certain.

Be deliberate about the layers that do not deflate. The orchestration that turns raw model output into reliable work, and the evidence record of what the agents actually did and why, are where value concentrates and where switching costs are genuinely real. That is the relationship to think hard about — and in a regulated context, the evidence layer is also your liability surface. Handing it to a velocity-optimized vendor means handing away both the durable margin and the part you are accountable for.

The honest counter-argument

The strongest case against all of this is vertical integration, and an argument that can’t hold it is propaganda. The frontier labs are not sitting still at the model layer. They are building up into the workflow — their own coding agents, their own editors, their own orchestration. If a model company also owns the harness and the workflow, the “value migrates up, away from the labs” thesis weakens sharply, because the labs migrate up with it. The reported moves around Cursor and the labs’ own coding tools are exactly this maneuver.

So the honest version of the argument is not that the labs are doomed to commoditization. It is that value migrates toward workflow and trust, and the open contest is who occupies that layer — the incumbent platforms that already hold developer identity and workflow, the labs moving up from the model, or the challengers climbing from the tool. That version is more useful precisely because it is falsifiable: you can watch who actually wins the workflow layer over the next few years and find out whether the thesis held.

There is a second honest limit. The verification and evidence layer is most defensible in regulated industries — automotive, finance, healthcare, government. For a startup shipping consumer software fast, the evidence layer matters far less, and the velocity-optimized frontier tooling may simply be the right answer. The argument is strongest where attestation is a legal requirement and weakest where speed is the only currency. Locating the buyer on that spectrum before deploying the argument is the difference between an insight and a script.

The disruption underneath it

There is a familiar shape under all of this. Incumbents tend to get attacked from below, by alternatives that start out “good enough” and cheaper rather than better. The small and edge-deployable models creeping up the capability curve are that disruptive entrant; the frontier labs are the incumbent holding the high-capability ground while being attacked on price from underneath. The labs’ defense — moving up into workflow and integration — is the textbook incumbent response. Reading the field this way doesn’t tell you who wins. It tells you what kind of contest it is, which is what lets you reason about it instead of reacting to each week’s headline.

Announced vs. demonstrated. This is an argument about the direction value moves in a platform shift, drawn from how earlier ones played out. The direction is the defensible claim; the timing and the eventual winner of the workflow layer are open and contestable. Treat it as a lens for deciding which supplier relationships to keep loose and which to invest in — not as a forecast of any company’s share price. Personal research, not investment advice.
