The economics of human-in-the-loop AI

A working model for when a single human question is worth more than it costs. April 2026.

There is a moment in most agentic AI sessions when a single well-placed question redirects the entire run. The question costs the human perhaps two minutes. It saves the system from completing a long, coherent, wrong path and then having to unwind it.

The economics of this are non-obvious because the cost of compute in LLM systems does not grow linearly. It grows quadratically with conversation length. Every API call re-sends the entire conversation history as input tokens. Turn 20 costs roughly twenty times more in input than turn 1. The human who intervenes at turn 8 is not just saving 8 turns — they are collapsing an entire compound cost curve.

This connects directly to the Ring 2 problem in multi-agent governance: the human is not a bottleneck when they intervene at the design level. They become a bottleneck only when the protocol layer is absent and they fill the gap by intervening at the execution level on every turn.

The equation

Total cost of an N-turn autonomous run:

C_total = (N × T_out × P_out + [N × T_sys + N(N+1)/2 × T_turn] × P_in) / 1,000,000

Where N = number of turns, T_sys = system prompt tokens, T_turn = average tokens added to context per turn, T_out = average output tokens per call, and P_in / P_out = price per million tokens for input and output respectively.

The critical term is N(N+1)/2 — the sum of an arithmetic series. Input cost grows triangularly. Double the turns and you roughly quadruple the input spend. Output is linear; input is not.

Net value of a human intervention at turn k that corrects a wrong-path run:

V = C_total(N) − C_total(k) − C_human
where C_human = (human_minutes / 60) × hourly_rate

When V > 0, the human's time is worth it on compute economics alone — before any quality adjustment for the value of arriving at the right answer.

What this means in practice

A one-question intervention that saves a 22-turn wrong-path run is worth roughly $0.40–$1.20 in compute for a mid-priced model — before factoring in the rework cost of arriving at the wrong answer and having to re-run. The economics favor early human direction strongly, especially as runs get longer.

This is not an argument for constant interruption. The lever is specificity: a question asked at the design level (“why are you using a hardcoded template when you already have a generation function?”) costs one exchange and redirects the architecture. A correction at the execution level (“change this variable name”) costs the same tokens but saves nothing structural.

The Ring 2 implication is this: if the protocol layer held the design intent clearly, the agent would have asked the question itself. Human intervention is the cost you pay when protocol is absent. Good Ring 2 design doesn't eliminate the human — it moves them upstream, where their time is worth the most and the quadratic curve hasn't had a chance to compound.
