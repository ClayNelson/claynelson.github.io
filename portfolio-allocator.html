Positioning against the barbell

A companion to the surplus model: if value migrates to the tips, what does a portfolio that believes it look like? June 2026.

The surplus-migration model answers where value lands as inference commoditizes. This one answers the next question: how would you position against that, in named equities, rather than in the abstract. It takes the same six levers and runs them through a set of real names, each carrying an exposure vector — its revenue split across the five layers.

The mechanism is the part worth understanding. A name’s attractiveness is its exposure vector dotted with where surplus currently sits. When you move a lever, the surplus vector shifts, and every name re-scores automatically — the ones exposed to layers gaining surplus rise, the ones selling into the squeezed middle fade. The suggested allocation is then a tilt over those scores, and it rolls back up into a layer view so you can read, in one bar, whether you are long the barbell or long the middle.

How to read the tilt

The conviction slider is what makes this an allocation tool rather than a ranking. At low conviction everything compresses toward equal-weight — the honest setting when you don’t trust the lever positions. At high conviction it concentrates hard into the names exposed to the winning layers — appropriate only when you believe the regime read. Conviction should track how much you trust the weakest link in the chain of assumptions, not how much you like the conclusion.

Two names repay attention. Push the SLM threshold to most and the edge-pure names and the workstation capex sleeve climb while the metered-middle names fade — the edge tip lighting up as small models clear real workloads. Then drop trust accrual to the floor and the harness and verification names sag even with verification weight high: the trust rate-limiter from the parent model propagates straight into position sizing, because realized harness work is what those names are exposed to.

Exposure vectors are theses, not measurements. Each name’s split across layers is a judgment about its revenue mix, set by hand and contestable. Nvidia is the most consequential call in the table — placed mostly in the metered middle on datacenter dominance, with a growing edge slice — and reasonable people would weight it differently. The allocator also scores names by alignment with where surplus sits, not by valuation: a name perfectly exposed to the winning layer can still be a poor buy if the market has already priced it. This sizes a tilt; it is not investment advice.
